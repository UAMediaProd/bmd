This module discusses the kinds of recreational drugs you might come across and the possible effects, as well as the legal implications. By the end of the module, you will be in a position to make informed choices about drug use and will have learnt some key harm reduction strategies.

Incorrect.

In most MDMA-related deaths, alcohol is also a contributing factor. When on MDMA, people feel like they have more energy and want to move or dance for long periods of time. MDMA also increases body temperature, and alcohol dehydrates the body. This combination increases the risk of heat stroke, which can be fatal. Additionally, alcohol reduces the MDMA 'high', meaning that people are more likely to overdose.

What should Chelsea's friends do to minimise harm while taking MDMA?

Stop drinking and take the entire pill.

Incorrect.

If they feel sure about taking the drug, they should ideally test the pill and check how much MDMA it contains. Even if the dose is low, they should all split the pills in (at least) half and then spread the doses throughout the night. Each person should gauge the dose depending on their weight (check the 'Useful links' pod to learn more about this). They should also stick together – staying with people that you trust is important while on MDMA. If someone is tripping alone, they could get very confused, lose track of time or have an accident. It's also important to drink water appropriately to prevent dehydration or water intoxication (excessive water intake) and take regular breaks from the dancefloor.

Chelsea's story shows how drugs can be used recreationally, but drugs are actually ever-present in daily life. Caffeine, paracetamol, alcohol, contraceptive pills... These substances are all classified as drugs because they cause a biological effect on the body when they are consumed.

Some drugs have immediate effects while others take time to act. Some have psychological effects and others do not. Some are legal or regulated, while others are illegal. What should you know before consuming any drug?

  • Dose: The amount of drug that should be taken at a given time
  • Onset of effects: The time it takes for the drug to trigger a biological change
  • Effects: The biological changes that are triggered by the drug, including physical and psychological changes
  • Interactions: The way the drug might interact with other drugs in your system
  • Method: How the drug is being consumed
  • Duration: The length of time that the biological changes last in the body
  • Time in the system: The time it takes for your body to eliminate the drug.

Knowledge is power. The more you know about something, the better informed you are to make decisions. Read on to learn more about the different types of recreational drugs, so that you can make informed decisions about your own choices.

Drink spiking is when psychoactive drugs are added to drinks without consent or notice. These drugs are often used for sexual assault and robbery.

Drugs used for spiking are depressants or dissociatives. Some known examples are GHB and Rohypnol. These drugs make you feel drowsy, uncoordinated and unaware, and make the victim more suggestible and vulnerable. We'll learn more about their effects on the screen titled: Psychoactive rainbow.

You can follow these tips to reduce the chances of suffering from drink spiking:

  • Never leave your drinks unattended
  • If someone offers to get you a drink, go with them and watch it being prepared
  • Avoid sharing drinks
  • Stay with your friends and look out for each other.

If any of your friends disappears for a while or looks unexpectedly drunk or high, their drink may have been spiked. Keep a close eye on them, and don't leave them alone. The effects will most likely wear off after a few hours, but if you have concerns about their condition, call triple zero.
