 Self-harm is when we do something to harm ourselves, often as a means of coping with overwhelming feelings The thought of self-harm can build up over a longer period of time – days or weeks – or can happen quickly – over a few hours, or less Suicidal thoughts can include thoughts that only last a short period of time, through to strong and intense thoughts with a plan Sometimes suicidal thoughts can happen quickly – or impulsively – with little warning Most people who have suicidal thoughts do not want to die, but do not know how to live and cope with their feelings Fearing being judged or making people angry can be the biggest barriers to seeking help There are lots of self-help strategies for self-harm and suicidal thoughts that can really make a big difference A safety plan can be one of the biggest and most positive things to do to create a supportive resource in times of difficulty Talking with a trained person can make a huge difference and can help break the cycle of self-harm, or deal with suicidal thinking.

It is important to remember that there is hope and even the most difficult thoughts can be changed for the better.

If you are experiencing suicidal thoughts, please contact one of the crisis support services that are available 24/7:

  • Contact Lifeline online or by calling 13 11 14
  • Contact the Suicide Call Back Service online or by calling 1300 659 467

Also, please consider using a suicide safety planning app:

  • Beyond Now
  • ReMinder

Your university might also have an out-of-hours crisis support line. Check with the health or counselling services at your university to find out.
