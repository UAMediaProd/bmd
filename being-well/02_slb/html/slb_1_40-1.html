Getting good sleep, and enough of it, is fundamental to functioning well in many aspects of life, including concentration, energy, mood and mental health. Given its importance, attitudes towards and knowledge about sleep can be surprisingly poor, as if it is a throw-away commodity that most of us can choose to do without. In reality, sleep is the mechanism that helps the body and the mind to repair, renew and refresh, consolidating memories and learning. If you have a great lecture in which you learn something new, it will stay with you better after a good night's sleep.

Sleep is something that most of us expect to happen without having to think about it. This leads to some assumptions about sleep that need looking at further.

It is not possible to fully 'repay' a sleep debt.

 True

While you cannot ever catch up in an absolute sense, the closer in time you can make any extra sleep to the lost sleep, the better. So if you have worked into the early hours on one night because of a deadline, then try to catch a little extra sleep the next day. But if you repeatedly miss out on sleep over a period of even one week, the effect can't be written off by, for example, sleeping in on the weekend.

True

True

Few people have perfect sleep all of the time. How well you sleep depends on lots of things, from genes to learned patterns of sleeping, to what's happening in your life in this moment. Sleep patterns can be disrupted by lots of things – working shifts, young children, 'all-nighters' and persistent disruptions caused by the sleeping environment.

Poor sleep can affect several areas of life, in ways that are not always obvious. Sometimes it is only the people around the sleep-deprived person who can really see what's going on, whilst the person themselves might feel they are coping OK. Or they may see the problem very well, but not be sure what they can do to solve it.

 Mood Poor sleep can be caused by stress, anxiety and depression. But it can also contribute to these issues by reducing resilience and perspective. Sleep is an important part of recovery for the body and the mind. Memory Your brain consolidates the day's learning while you sleep. Doing this requires neurones that are refreshed and efficient – and this is what good sleep will give you. Concentration Disturbed or insufficient sleep reduces your ability to give something your full attention. This affects everything from researching a dissertation to cooking a meal – it will take longer and be harder to do. Performance Persistent tiredness affects the ability to assess a situation and respond to it appropriately. You won't do as well as you can when you can't think clearly. Health Persistent poor sleep can lower the immune system and lead to lower energy levels, and more coughs and colds. Being fit and well includes giving yourself time to sleep. Relationships Sleep impacts on mood, memory, concentration, performance, and health. All of these things impact on relationships in the long run because sleep-deprived people are often more irritable and less patient.

Of course, sleep may not always be the cause – or the answer – to some of these issues. But it is a possible contributor, just as it is a possible solution. Knowing your own sleep patterns, and thinking about the areas that poor sleep can commonly affect, is a good starting point to taking action to improve things.
