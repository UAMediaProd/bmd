Alcohol can have a surprisingly strong effect on the body and mind, even when drunk in moderation.

Brain
 Alcohol is a depressant drug. It slows down parts of the brain. It targets the GABA receptor, which sends messages around the nervous system to reduce activity of the nerve cells. Therefore, it calms the brain down by making fewer neurons fire. The areas of the brain that are affected are involved in: control inhibition, thought, perception, attention, judgement, memory, sleep and coordination.
Hangover
 When we drink, the chemistry of the brain changes. The way in which nerve cells talk to each other is affected and they try to compensate for it. Alcohol can make you cheerful and relaxed. This means that the following day you end up with the opposite: hangover and anxiety. The body needs some time to go back to its usual levels. The only way to avoid a hangover is to drink less. Unfortunately, there is not much one can do once the alcohol is in the body. Drinking plenty of water, sleeping enough and taking painkillers are the best remedies.
Interactions
 When different drugs are consumed together, the resulting effects can be unexpected. Mixing recreational and medicinal drugs with alcohol can have negative consequences. Alcohol usually combines badly with most drugs. If you are taking any medicinal drugs, read the prescription and follow any information about alcohol. Caffeine, which is found in most energy drinks, can also pose a health risk when combined with alcohol. Mixing these drugs in drinks such as JÃ¤gerbombs puts extra stress on the heart and liver.
Mind
 The positive effects of alcohol include relaxation, happiness and increased sociability. The neutral effects of alcohol include slurred speech, flushed skin, sleepiness and blurry vision. The negative effects of alcohol include decreased coordination, blackouts and memory loss at high levels, dizziness, emotional volatility and a reduced ability to judge your own level of impairment. The effects start to kick in around 15-30 minutes after starting to drink.
Decisions
 Drinking alcohol can modify our priorities and decisions in the short- and long-term. When drunk, it is easier to misjudge a situation and people often take more risks – for example, starting fights or saying something offensive or cruel due to lowered inhibitions. This can affect relationships and the strength of our social networks. Because alcohol is generally expensive, developing an unhealthy drinking habit could also result in spending much more than planned on a night out.

In Australia, we have a simple way to find out how much alcohol we are drinking – it's called a 'standard drink'. It is equal to 10g of alcohol.

Standard drinks are a simple way of working out the quantity of pure alcohol in a drink. Different countries have different standards, so you need to use the Australian measures. Standard drinks are calculated using the following formula: The volume (L) × The strength of the alcohol (ABV/%) × 0.789..

Alcohol can make us relaxed, happy and sociable, but we can also feel dizzy, emotionally volatile and uncoordinated. The day after drinking moderate to high levels of alcohol we might feel anxious and in a low mood. The only way to avoid this is to drink less. Luckily, we can easily track how much we are drinking by counting the number of standard drinks in the drinks that we consume (see the 'Useful links' pod for details).
