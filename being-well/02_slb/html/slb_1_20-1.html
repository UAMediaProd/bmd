University can provide exciting opportunities to try new things and develop different parts of your identity. Sometimes the problem can be managing to fit it all in, and working out what's important for both your academic and your personal life. Each student is unique, and finding the right balance for you is important.

 Overcommitment

Overcommitting can leave you feeling unsatisfied and anxious, or simply overtired, as you try to do it all. What's more, we live in bodies that just won't work like machines. Overtiredness will impact both efficiency and enjoyment – it takes longer to get things done, we do them less well and don't enjoy them as much. Remember to:

  • Prioritise – keep sight of what's important, and be prepared to let go of things that aren't
  • Distinguish between habits and priorities, and don't confuse the two
  • Plan and follow through on the plan
  • Review both priorities and plans, to make sure they are up to date and working
  • Balance – get the work, play and rest balance right.
 Indecisiveness

This will happen more when you are not clear about your priorities. This can be for many reasons – perhaps not knowing yourself well enough yet, feeling pressured from outside to take things on, or being unclear about where you want your life to go. Remember:

  • If you keep putting something off; consider whether you really want to do it!
  • If you have a tendency to procrastinate, try making a list so you can see your plans written down and check them off for a sense of achievement
  • Focus on the next thing that needs doing to meet your goals – breaking them down into smaller actions helps to get a clearer idea of what you actually have to do. Completing a degree becomes attending the next lecture; playing team football becomes attending the next practice session, etc.
 Can't say "no"

At university there is no one to monitor your activities except you. Being able to say "no" is a crucial skill. Often called 'being assertive', it doesn't mean ignoring what other people need or want; it means counting what you need and want as equally important. A good start is:

  • Be honest. "I'm sorry but I just can't help at the moment" is a perfectly OK thing to say. You don't have to justify it
  • Say what you can do as well as what you can't: "I can't do that right now but how about next week?"

This means that you are more able to honour the commitments that are truly priorities for you; and that must include being aware of your own wellbeing. The module titled: Your student journey explores the importance of maintaining an awareness of your own priorities.

 Pinch points

These are the times when deadlines coincide. Consider these students and their conflicting commitments:

  • It's a month till the summer break and exam period. Mike has four exams in the next four weeks. He is getting invitations to end of semester parties and has also committed to a new job at a local restaurant
  • It's the end of Ramadan. Maalik's family is expecting him home for the Eid celebration, but he has not told them that he has a presentation due on the first day of Eid, and an important athletics match the day after that.

How will Mike and Maalik manage? The real question is, was any of this unforeseeable? The answer is no. Looking ahead and planning for these pinch points would have been the best way of dealing with them. At university, forward planning is crucial, and with it you will accomplish much more, with less stress.

 Fitting in paid work

Many students need paid work in order to manage their finances. Most universities recommend doing no more than 15 hours per week (and international students may have visa restrictions to consider), and even this might be difficult depending on your year of study. To help manage the tensions between paid work and study, remember:

  • Employers value reliability and will often be flexible if you can tell them ahead of time when you won't be available
  • Increasing your paid hours when there are fewer deadlines can help you manage when you have to pull back in the more pressured times
  • Working during the vacation and saving the money can reduce reliance on term-time work.

John is a law student in his second year, and has problems trying to fit everything in

Jiao is a first-year drama student who feels that she is always doing things for others and not for herself

Anna is a full-time Master's student torn between her work and family commitments

 John received a minimum pass mark for his latest assignment because he missed the deadline despite doing an all-nighter, and he wants to avoid this happening again. He had a bunch of things due at once – three assignments over two weeks, chairing a debate as part of the Law Society, preparing for a voluntary work placement interview over the Easter holidays and putting his application in to be next year's part-time Students' Association Societies Officer. John could improve the situation by learning better time management skills. If he can manage his time more efficiently, his schedule would look more organised, without limiting his enthusiasm and ambition. John's problem is over-commitment – he is trying to do too many things. He needs to prioritise and limit himself to the things that are really important to him.

Feedback

It is possible that better time management skills would help; for example, making plans for dealing with multiple deadlines. However, John's schedule does seem packed, and he is planning to work in a position that will leave him with even less time next year, when the study workload is higher. Only seeing it as time management might reinforce a feeling that he should be able to manage things if he only tried harder. Time management will need to include a realistic appraisal of priorities and objectives, an assessment of his expectations of himself, and reducing commitments where he can.

Feedback

Looking at John's commitments, it does seem that he has taken a lot on. It may be challenging for him to consider this, as he is likely to feel that everything is important! There is a great deal of pressure on students to engage in opportunities to enhance their CV in a competitive employment market, and John may feel he has little choice but to take every opportunity. Yet it is impacting his results, which are his priority. He may benefit from sitting down with a careers advisor to get some information about what employers look for so that he can focus on that. A chat with a friend could help him understand his personal priorities while not losing sight of the things that he enjoys. A wellbeing advisor may help him with thinking about how he can look after his mental health in the long run; burnout is a possibility here, especially with the increased workload in his final year. With a more realistic schedule and better planning, he can get back on track.

Return to menu Anna and her partner, Naomi, have two young children. They have agreed that Anna should go back to university and do a Master's degree to enhance her career prospects. Naomi has taken additional responsibility for childcare; nevertheless, Anna is feeling that she never has time to do anything properly, and wonders if the whole thing was a mistake. It may help Anna to take a break from studies and reassess whether this is what she really wants to do. She could consider returning when the children are older, or moving to do her degree part-time. The shift to being a student has been more difficult than Anna expected, and she and Naomi may need to renegotiate how they manage this. She could also talk to her subject tutor and see whether the department can offer any help.

Feedback

While taking a break is a possibility, it is likely to be premature at this stage. The problem is not studying for the degree itself, but finding a way to combine it with her family commitments, and there is room for Anna to adjust her expectations of herself as both a parent and a student. She could consult her tutor and student services to help her to build solutions around these competing demands – they will want to help her find a way forward.

Feedback

It is only to be expected that things are tougher than Anna or Naomi imagined, and it is natural for them to need to make more, or different, adjustments to those that they anticipated. Now that they have a clearer understanding of the demands on Anna's time, they are in a good position to make some new plans. For example, Anna can review her time management skills so that she makes the most of the time that she has, and can also check for any perfectionism that leads her towards unrealistic goals. Student services or study skills advisors will be able to help with this, as will the department, who will be used to supporting mature students with family commitments. They may be able to offer more flexibility than she realises. Connecting with other mature-age students can be a good way to look at how others in a similar position are managing their study and family commitments.
