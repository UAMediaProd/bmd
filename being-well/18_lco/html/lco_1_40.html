Yasmin has stopped at the supermarket to buy her main meal for the day. She has some ingredients at home but doesn't feel like cooking, even though she is low on money. What should she buy?

 A supermarket home brand ready meal Ready meals are quick and easy, but will be more expensive in the long run. They may not be the best nutritional choice either. Yasmin should save these for when she is really busy. Nothing. Get a takeaway Tempting and convenient, but expensive! An occasional takeaway is a treat and can brighten a bad day, but buy them too often and Yasmin's budget will be swallowed up. A new pasta sauce that's on special A great option, as Yasmin will save some money and she can cook some of the pasta she has at home. She could add some vegetables that are on special too for a quick, relatively healthy meal. She may even have some leftovers for later in the week. A tin of beans and a loaf of bread Beans on toast is great, cheap, comfort food but may not be sufficient as a main meal. Will Yasmin use the bread up over the week or will it go to waste?

Iqbal has just been sent his mobile phone bill, but he can't afford to pay it. He got the contract before he came to university, and even though the phone is old, the bill is still $40 a month. Does Iqbal have any options?

 Yes, he can just stop paying his contract Incorrect. An easy option, but if Iqbal stops paying a contract without notice, he will be in breach of the contract and could end up being chased for non-payment or, in the worst case, be referred to a debt collector. He should talk to the organisation immediately and discuss payment options. No, he's tied into his contract and will get a new, even better phone soon anyway Maybe. The contract terms and conditions may include a clause for early termination. Iqbal should work out the cost of leaving the contract against the cost of moving to a cheap pay-as-you-go phone for the rest of the contract term, and whether the replacement phone will meet his needs. Yes, he can try and switch or renegotiate his contract It's always worth talking to the mobile phone provider about alternatives mid-contract, especially if affordability is an issue. Iqbal may have to swap to an older handset, but he could save a lot of money. Yes, he can keep the phone but will need to make savings elsewhere Maybe. Iqbal can't see himself with a different phone – his whole life is on this device! When something is such a priority, then savings need to be found elsewhere. He could review outgoings for other non-essential payments, such as streaming services, and weigh up which is more important.

It's the end of term and Ben's housemates want to go out to town to celebrate. Ben is already in his overdraft and can't really afford it, but he doesn't want to let his friends down or miss out on a good night. Should he go out?

 Yes, it will be worth it and his pay comes in next week Incorrect. By continuing to build up his overdraft, when Ben's next pay comes in it will just go towards paying the overdraft and he will be no better off. He needs to keep his overdraft to a minimum and try to increase his income. No, he should say he's not feeling well and stay at home Incorrect. Whilst staying at home is a good option, being dishonest about the reason is not a great idea. Apart from anything else it might make him feel even worse. Ben should talk to his friends about his situation. They may come up with a more affordable way to celebrate, that includes everyone. Yes, but he should tell his friends that he can only spend a minimum amount This way, Ben can still go out, but his friends will know that he needs to be careful about what he's spending. They could check out places with student offers to keep costs down. No, he needs to be honest with his friends and himself Maybe. Being honest is a good policy. It is likely that others are in the same boat and this may prompt a discussion about whether they can have an alternative celebration that would be cheaper all round.

Jo has received an email offering a free TV streaming subscription for three months. Should she sign up?

 Yes, what's there to lose? Incorrect. It looks like a great offer and it's free – or is it? Jo must check the terms and conditions and whether her bank or card details are needed in order to sign up. If so, Jo can expect to be charged at the end of the 'free' period unless she cancels within the stated notice period. No, this is a con and it will mean she gets caught in a contract Maybe. It's always a good idea to check out 'free offers' and make sure they are legitimate. If it is legitimate, there may well be a catch. Jo should check how long she is signing up for, whether she can cancel and how much she has to pay if she doesn't cancel. Yes, she can review her use before she has to start paying If Jo is smart she can sign up, put a reminder in her diary to cancel before the fees are due, and make sure she cancels any automatic payments she may have set up. These offers are designed to make it hard to give up the freebie. Can Jo afford the subscription if she decides she can't live without the service? No, she can't afford it when the charges start Maybe. Whilst the offer is tempting and appears to be free, Jo knows that she can't afford the extra fortnightly fee based on her current commitments. If she signs up she might be tempted not to cancel at the end of the free period and then her finances will be pushed to the limit.

It's late at night, and Talia is feeling low and can't sleep. She has received an email alerting her to a price reduction on an expensive pair of trainers that she had considered buying a few weeks ago. Should she buy them now?

 Yes, it will cheer her up Incorrect. Talia is feeling low and she hasn't had much sleep. A decision to suddenly buy an expensive item is likely to be one that she will regret, especially when she sees her bank balance tomorrow. If impulse purchasing is something you are prone to, try and understand the triggers and adopt strategies to help prevent you from making expensive decisions. No, she didn't need them a few weeks ago so doesn't need them now This is the rational answer, but research (Murray, N. 2017) has shown that impulse purchases are more likely to occur when someone is feeling low. It is also late at night, when there are fewer distractions. Talia should try to work out a strategy for dealing with these situations, as she is prone to impulse spending. Yes, she can always return them if she changes her mind Maybe. Talia may feel strong enough to return the trainers when she receives them and has had a longer opportunity to consider the purchase. Research (Murray, 2017) suggests that shoppers struggle to return purchases they later regret. No, she should wait until the morning at least Maybe. It is a good tactic to stall a hasty purchase decision, which Talia may regret later. Talia will have had time to think about the financial consequences of making this purchase and whether she can afford the trainers. Any strategy that prevents impulse buying and gives you time to think can be beneficial in sticking to your budget.
