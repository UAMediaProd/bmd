Before applying for any type of loan, make sure you have all the necessary information to hand. Depending on the type of loan, this will include your full name, date of birth (DOB), address, and any current employment and financial commitments, such as other loans or mortgages. Take the time to carefully read each question and answer it fully.

The company you are applying to will be able to check some of the information you supply by undertaking a credit score or credit rating check, which will form an important part of their decision-making. A credit search is a check made by a lender using a credit reference agency to view an applicant's credit history. See the screen titled: Credit ratings (or credit score) for more details.

 Loans and borrowing options

There are a number of options available if you need to borrow money, with some carrying more risk than others. If you are an international student and wish to use a credit card while in Australia, some local financial institutions will allow you to make an application if you have ongoing employment. Alternatively, you can apply for a credit card in your home country before arriving.

 Overdrafts
  • Overdrafts can be attached to an everyday transaction account. You'll be approved for a set amount and able to access these funds when all other funds in your account have been exhausted. You may have to pay a fee to set up the overdraft and you will pay interest on the amount you use
  • Pros: Relatively cheap form of borrowing and can be made available quite quickly
  • Cons: You must stay within the agreed limit to avoid unnecessary charges. Exceeding a limit could negatively affect your credit score. You may also have to pay account fees.
 Credit cards
  • Traditionally one of the most popular methods of borrowing, these allow users to spend money up to an agreed credit limit, and pay it back incrementally. They can be used with all retailers, and some cards offer additional protections such as refund or purchase protection
  • Pros: Widely accepted form of secure payment, including contactless
  • Cons: Very easy to run up large debts, and interest charges can be up to 22% APR. Only to be considered if you can comfortably meet the required monthly repayments.
 Payday loans
  • Also called a 'small amount loan'. Lets you borrow up to $2,000, to be repaid between 16 days and one year. Interest cannot be charged but most lenders charge an establishment fee of 20% of the loan amount and then a monthly service fee of 4% of the amount borrowed
  • Pros: Online application, immediate approval and same-day payment
  • Cons: High fees, expensive form of borrowing and should be treated as a last resort. Always seek advice on these (try your university's student services) before applying. Late payment can affect your credit score.
 Apps
  • Some apps (such as Afterpay, Zip Pay and Klarna) allow people to purchase from retailers but pay later
  • Pros: Accessible, and quick and easy to use
  • Cons: Can encourage people to spend beyond their means and result in unmanageable debts.

Some people also prefer to borrow money from or lend money to friends or family. There are pros and cons to this type of borrowing – it may be cheaper than commercial lending, but it can cause issues if agreements are not kept.

Our natural inclination is likely to be to help a friend or family member in need, but lending money to someone you know can cause problems if the terms are not properly agreed. There is likely to be bad feeling if the money is not repaid in full, or in the time promised, which could damage the relationship. Think carefully before lending any money, and ensure there is a written agreement that states the expectations of both parties. Equally, if you are planning to borrow money from someone, think carefully about if you will be able to repay on time, and in full, and be honest about your situation.

 Your context Your context

Your university will have numerous options to support students who are struggling financially. A good place to start is checking the students' association website, or speaking with the student services office who will be able to direct you to the right person to help.

With each type of lending, it is important to ensure you can make repayments on time.

If you (or someone you know) are struggling with repayments, you could contact your university or students' association to ask what advice and help may be available. If they are unable to help, they may be able to refer you to a community organisation for support.
