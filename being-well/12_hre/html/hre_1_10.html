Sadly, we are often able to more easily describe the features of negative relationships than we are healthy, positive relationships. Being a bystander to a difficult incident or situation is an uncomfortable position to be in, and it can be hard to know what to do. Taking the role of a positive bystander doesn't have to mean taking a huge stand or intervening in a violent situation. It can be as simple as a quiet word at the right time. Being empathetic and non-judgemental is key to being a positive bystander. We will look at some skill-building activities throughout this module to help you be a confident and effective positive bystander. We'll also give you some resources you can call on if you, or someone you know, are in an unhealthy relationship. Would you feel comfortable saying something to a friend if you saw them treating someone badly in a relationship? What might you say? This is a really hard one and being a positive bystander should never place you or anyone else in an unsafe situation. Again, it can be as simple as saying the right thing in the right way. Having difficult conversations with friends about their behaviour is never going to be something we want to do. But, we know we have to because we don't want them to harm others or themselves. Without someone who cares about them saying something, they may not realise their behaviour is harmful. It could be the catalyst for them to change and create a healthy relationship in future.
