Moderate physical activity includes exercise that raises your heart rate, makes you breathe faster and makes you feel warmer. Which one of the following physical activities do you think fits into this category?

 Brisk walking or wheelchair use Ten-pin bowling Skipping rope Putting away groceries, cooking food

Official guidelines recommend at least 2.5 hours of moderate physical activity per week (Australian Government Department of Health, 2019). However, any amount of this exercise is good for you. This includes a range of physical activities such as walking, cleaning the house, gardening, tai chi, lifting and carrying – with many of these activities run inside if the weather is not suitable.

Vigorous physical activity makes you breathe hard and fast. If you are working at this level, you won't be able to say more than a few words without pausing for breath. Which of the following options represents vigorous physical activity?

 Swimming Softball Gardening Playing golf

Vigorous physical activity brings health benefits over and above that of moderate activity. In fact, half as much vigorous activity is needed to get similar benefits as from moderate exercise. Nonetheless, a combination of both types of activity is best so as not to place too much stress on the body. These activities should always be in response to the weather and with adequate hydration.

Alongside moderate and vigorous physical activities, muscle-strengthening exercises help to maintain health and wellbeing. Strength-based exercises work all the major muscles (legs, hips, back, abdomen, chest, shoulders and arms). From the following options, select the activity which directly builds strength:

 Yoga Playing basketball Tennis Mild stretching

Muscle-strengthening exercises include a variety of activities that need to be performed safely and with adequate rest and recovery. These may include lifting weights, working with resistance bands, yoga, active gardening (such as digging and shovelling), hill walking and dancing.

Connection between mental and physical health in students

University students who take part in regular physical activity perform better, are more employable and enjoy better mental wellbeing.For Indigenous students, this is even more important, as the challenges to accessing culturally safe health services can be significant barriers to equal healthcare (Australian Institute of Health and Welfare, 2016). Students in higher education are under more pressure than ever to balance studies, finances, and family and community obligations, and often have to manage associated feelings of loneliness and isolation. However, students who classified themselves as 'active' scored better in all aspects of personal wellbeing than those classified as 'fairly active' or 'inactive'.

Physical activity could help with depression

Orygen's Research Bulletin suggests that, for young people:

  • Engaging in physical exercise could decrease symptoms of depression
  • Regular exercise is similar in effect to psychological support
  • The benefits of exercise are both mental and physical and can lead to longer term health benefits.

(Orygen, Bulletin 10)

Engaging in regular exercise leads to higher levels of optimism

For substantial gains in physical and mental health, adults should aim to do 150 minutes of physical activity at a moderate to vigorous intensity per week. You should also focus on muscle-strengthening exercises two times per week. Movement is a major part of health care, and it is especially important to break up the time we spend sitting down.

(Physical Activity Australia, 2015)
