No one can make themselves sleep well – in fact, lying there worrying about not sleeping is only likely to make it worse! We all have times when we just can't sleep. But everyone can make changes to help create the right conditions for sleeping.

Noise and sleep

Having a quiet environment is essential for good sleep – we are built to wake up with noise. If you like to fall asleep to gentle music, just set it to fade out at some point. If you share a bedroom, then use earphones. Some noises like your neighbour's music or the party in the flat below you can't always control – so invest in a good pair of earplugs, which can work wonders. Be assertive with neighbours about what you need, and find out what they need too.

Switch off from work

If possible, don't use your bed as a workspace at all – your mind needs a clear separation between work and sleep space. Make a to-do list for tomorrow, put away books and notes, and close down work files on your computer. You are giving your brain the message that it can switch off and relax. Make an agreement with a room-sharer that if they want to work late they will use another room, like the kitchen, and be prepared to do the same yourself. You need a good hour or two to change gear before you go to sleep. A warm bath or shower is a good idea, or very gentle exercise.

Switch off from social media

Put charging items somewhere out of reach and turn notifications off to lessen the temptation to check them when you are preparing for sleep. Back-lit items like phones, tablets and laptops use blue light which is proven to impact the quality of sleep. Another good reason not to check your devices until you are ready to wake up!

Falling asleep

You need to commit to sleep, and get into a routine. Try to get into the habit of turning any media off at least half an hour (preferably more) before you go to bed. Put work away. Close the door. Change into your sleeping clothes (if you wear them) and get under the duvet. Some people find it helpful to do a short relaxation exercise. Turn all the lights out except your bedside light. Have your earplugs and eye mask to hand. Read a book, listen to calming music or a relaxing podcast.

If you wake up

If you wake up in the middle of your sleep, don't worry, it's natural. Try not to look at the clock immediately – this is likely to wake you up more. If you really can't sleep it's OK to get out of bed, quietly make a warm (decaffeinated) drink, read or listen to something gentle (no blue light) and try again.

Caffeine and other stimulants

Caffeine and other stimulants work against sleep; this includes most coffee, tea and fizzy drinks unless they say otherwise. The same goes for nicotine in cigarettes. Try and have at least four hours clear before you go to sleep as caffeine takes a long time to leave the body.

Alcohol and sleep

Alcohol is a depressant, and as such it can have a sedative effect. However, you will start to wake up again when the alcohol wears off, and it disrupts the quality of your sleep. Don't drink in order to sleep – use some of the other techniques described in this activity for a more healthy long-term approach.

Food and sleep

Generally, it's good to have had your main meal several hours before going to bed. To avoid waking up hungry in the night, having a light snack closer to bedtime is fine. Milk, oats and rice are all good for sleep. If you can't sleep and there is a cold pizza by the bed you may be tempted to eat it! So clear the room of food leftovers before you go to bed.

Lights out

Because the body responds to light, the room in which we sleep needs to be dark, and preferably cool (ideally 16–18C, or 60–65F). If you can get black-out curtains, so much the better, but if you can't then invest in a good eye mask. Reduce other light sources where possible, paying especial attention to blue light.

Sleep is an important aspect of staying well. Just like food and exercise, it is part of what your body and mind need in order to deal positively with the demands of each day. No one sleeps perfectly all the time, so don't stress over it if you have a few bad nights. Do see whether any of these tips work for you, and if the problem continues for a month or more then consider seeking advice from your GP.
