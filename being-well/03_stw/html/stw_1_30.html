Procrastination – putting off something that needs to be done – is something we can all recognise from time to time, albeit in different ways and in response to different situations. For some people procrastination can be relatively fleeting and easily resolvable while, for others, it can become a pattern of behaviour that is evident in many personal and professional situations.

I used to find all sorts of ways of putting off the things I didn't want to do, like finish an essay. I went to a free session organised by student services about combating procrastination, and the tips were helpful. I also find that giving myself rewards for completing tasks, like a night out with my mates, is a great motivator!

At the end of the year my current housemate decided to move out. The thought of finding someone to live with next year was really worrying me, so I just buried my head in the sand because there was no one who seemed an obvious choice. My friend suggested asking around at some of the societies I'm a member of, so I asked at my next footy match if anyone needed a housemate. One of the guys said his housemate was graduating and there was a room going in his house. I went round to see it and it was exactly what I needed, and such a weight off my mind to get my accommodation sorted.

Ben, 20

I knew I had this exam to do but I really didn't understand the material. The task seemed almost too big to deal with, so I avoided it. I was really struggling and mentioned as much to my friend after class. She said she was finding it difficult too, and suggested we go over the material to work it out together. We set up a few study sessions and managed to use our shared knowledge to get a better grip of the material. It was so helpful to know someone else was having the same concerns, and working as a team felt much more manageable than going through it all on my own.

Estelle, 31

 Arranging to meet up with friends or course mates at a social event The thought might be: Everyone is doing better/is more popular/is more interesting than me and so they will find me boring. This type of procrastination is a fear of being judged and ultimately reinforces the fear because the person does not allow their fears to be proved wrong. Arranging to meet with your subject tutor or lecturer The thought might be: They will think I am not good enough for my course/not contributing enough/I will sound stupid when I talk to them. This can often be linked to 'imposter syndrome', where we feel that we are not as good as others think we might be and we are in the position from good luck, rather than good planning or hard work. Again, avoiding the situation reinforces the fear as the thoughts are never challenged. Imposter syndrome is explored in the module titled: Postgraduate research student wellbeing. Not attending lectures The thought might be: There's little point in attending as I don't learn anything new and find it quite boring. We often need some energy to be able to undertake tasks, e.g. an interest or motivation in a subject. If it is hard to find that energy, perhaps due to disinterest, we can quickly begin to put it off and then withdraw. Preparing a revision timetable The thought might be: I have left this far too late to do any meaningful revision. Sometimes we can have the best plans to be prepared and get going but other activities get in the way and we find ourselves running out of time. Feeling that little can be achieved – 'what's the point?' – can lead us to put this off even more.

As we can see, procrastination can present in many ways and in response to different situations. However, while we separated out personal and study-related issues to consider the thoughts behind them, we can see that the thoughts cross these boundaries – the way we think about ourselves personally will be significant in how we think about our studies, and vice versa.
