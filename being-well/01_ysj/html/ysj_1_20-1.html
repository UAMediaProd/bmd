Peer support Peer support

Students are much more likely to share their difficulties with fellow students than any other source of support.

If you have a friend who shares their difficulties with you, don't feel you need to have all the answers to 'fix' the problem. Just listening and showing that you accept how they are feeling can be a lot more helpful than giving your opinion or trying to advise them.

While people's social media posts tend to focus on sharing their 'best life', it can also be a huge comfort when friends are honest about their own struggles, and don't pretend to have all the answers.

Grace got the ATAR results she was predicted and a place at her first choice of university. She was popular at school, and feels confident and excited about university. Grace comes from a regional town and has moved interstate for university. She is living out of home for the first time, sharing private accommodation with other students. She is completely unprepared when she experiences strong pangs of homesickness during her first few weeks at university.

Parents: We don't know what to say to Grace when she phones home in floods of tears, saying she wants to come home for the weekend. It's not like her at all. We just try to remind her how excited she was, and tell her not to be so silly when she says no one likes her. So far we've managed to persuade her to stay there on weekends – running back home isn't going to help her get to know people and settle in.

Friends from home: From the pictures she'd posted on social media we thought Grace was having a fantastic time at university. Then she sent a drunken message to our old Year 12 group chat saying worrying things like: "What's the point?" but she deleted it the next morning so we didn't want to embarrass her by bringing it up. We're all going to go back home in a few weeks and we've organised a big party. Hopefully that will cheer her up.

Our advice

Homesickness isn't 'silly' – it's a natural emotion that many people experience. The powerful combination of anxiety and sadness is triggered as a signal that we are lacking the familiar surroundings and close, bonded relationships which are essential to our long-term safety. So it's almost inevitable to experience some level of homesickness when starting in a completely new and unfamiliar environment.

Grace could:

  • Let her parents know that they can help by acknowledging rather than dismissing her feelings
  • Organise visits during university breaks to give herself the rest and connection she needs whilst she is still finding her feet at university
  • Join in as much as possible with O-Week activities, but focus on activities she enjoys to give herself the best chance of meeting like-minded people to make friends with.

Our advice

It is understandable to want our social media posts to give the impression of a confident, popular image. But staying properly in touch with old friends – with everyone sharing the ups and downs of their new challenges – can be a really valuable source of support while you are finding your feet at university.

Grace's friends could help by:

  • Expressing their concern about her post and encouraging her to talk honestly about how she is feeling
  • Not assuming what would cheer her up, but asking her what would help most
  • Sharing their own struggles and difficulties in adjusting to new environments, where relevant.

Leon could:

Our advice

Living in shared accommodation can be a great source of friendship and fun, and an opportunity to get to know a wide range of people from differing backgrounds – but this does also come with challenges. You will almost certainly need to develop skills for expressing and accommodating a range of needs and preferences. Simon's housemates may not be taking into account factors like financial constraints, which might impact his choices.

Simon could:

  • Consider sharing his needs and preferences with his housemates in a more constructive way
  • See if his university has information or frameworks for students engaging in rental accommodation (see the screen titled: Forming communities for more information)

Our advice

Household agreements can help to set expectations at the beginning stages of renting. Things like noise should be discussed for everyone's benefit and there should be backup ideas if things don't go to plan. Many universities offer rental advice to students, as well as support for dealing with complaints and moving if things become too difficult in their current living arrangements.

Simon could explore other university support options such as:

  • Proactive support for him and his housemates to develop inclusive and respectful relationships, such as existing or facilitated housemate agreements
  • Mediation or conflict resolution services to help students resolve common disputes, in shared accommodation or group work, for example
  • Specific support for students from out-of-home care, or those who are estranged from or lacking family support, which could include additional financial or priority access to suitable accommodation and specialist mentoring.
