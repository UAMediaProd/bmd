Good study skills are often based on developing helpful habits, which we can repeat and embed in our daily lives. Successful study does not sit in isolation; the more we are able to take care of our mental health and physical wellbeing generally, the more able we will be to study successfully and reach our potential.

Emotional/psychological support

Doing things that help us feel good, such as:

  • Taking time out to relax and rest
  • Practising positive self-talk (see the module titled: Am I good enough? for more information)
  • Reminding ourselves of our achievements and successes
  • Using mindfulness or relaxation techniques (see the module titled: Stress, anxiety and depression for ideas)
  • Listening to music we enjoy
  • Reading (non-study) books and magazines
  • Talking to a trusted friend or family member
  • Seeking support from university support services
  • Using apps or websites to guide meditation and improve focus (see the 'Useful links' pod for more information).

Behavioural support

Seeking advice or support from a number of sources, such as:

  • Spending time with friends and loved ones
  • Setting up or attending a study group
  • Ensuring regular attendance at lectures and tutorials
  • Working in public spaces, e.g. the library, to help avoid feelings of isolation
  • Not working for any longer than 45 minutes at a stretch, as we know this is the optimal time for concentration before we begin to lose focus
  • Taking a 15-minute break between each 'block' of work
  • Developing and reviewing work timetables
  • Accessing study skills support within the institution (see the module titled: Study-life balance for more information).

Bodily support

Supporting your body by eating well or exercising, including:

  • Spending time in natural environments – e.g. visiting a national park or bushwalking
  • Eating well (see the module titled: Eating well for information)
  • Developing and maintaining good sleep strategies (see the module titled: Study-life balance for advice)
  • Exercising (even a brisk walk can work wonders – see the module titled: Being active for more ideas)
  • Breathing techniques to calm the body and mind, e.g. slowly breathing in on a count of three, then out on a count of three, then repeating for two minutes
  • Paying attention to body 'signals' of stress, such as tight breathing, and using strategies to attend to them (see the module titled: Stress, anxiety and depression for information)
  • Checking out health concerns for your own peace of mind
  • Making sure you are comfortable in your study environment – e.g. the seating, desk, lighting and noise level.

 Your context Your context

Your institution will have a range of support services and information available to help with improving study skills and your own sense of wellbeing at university. Check your university's website or student services office for information.

Different things work for different people at different times. Developing a good routine for mental and physical wellbeing, as well as good study routines, is often a trial-and-error process. The build-up to exams and assessments can be particularly stressful. Good study strategies can help ease this phase.

I set a timer so I only work for 40 minutes at a time, with 10-minute breaks in-between. It really helps to keep my energy up and maximises my concentration so I don't become too tired. This enables me to plan a good revision timetable in the build-up to my exams.

Whilst the internet is a fantastic resource for studying, it can be a real distraction. Once I have all the information I need, I turn my WiFi off or unplug the internet – you can even use apps to help stop you using your phone for periods of time.

I find mindfulness incredibly helpful because it enables me to focus on the here and now. I use breathing techniques to 'unhook' myself from the things that are stressing me out and focus on my revision. Mindfulness is an approach that allows thinking to 'be in the moment', without judgement. As such, it can be extremely helpful in learning to be with stressful or challenging thoughts without ruminating on the past, or worrying about the future.

When I can, I try and organise my studies like a working day, say 8am to 5pm with breaks built in. This keeps me focused and allows me to organise my time better so that I can spend more time with my family and mob. This makes planning my time much easier and helps me think about what revision I can do, and when.

I try out different places to study to see which one works best for me: in the library, at home, in a café, etc. It depends on what I need to do, but finding the right space to work in is as important as anything else.

I know I am getting stressed when I 'throw' more time at my studies, working longer and longer hours. I often don't realise I am getting exhausted. I find less is more: working smarter rather than longer leads to better results in coursework, revision and exams.

Do any of these strategies appeal to you? Take a moment to consider how you could adapt them to work for you.

There are almost limitless ways of supporting yourself to develop good study routines. It's helpful to remember that effective and healthy study routines can play an important part in contributing to our positive sense of wellbeing, and vice versa. Try not to compare yourself to others, as comparison is often highly subjective and typically incorrect. However, do use other people's good ideas and try them out for yourself, though be aware that what works for someone else might not work for you.

 Useful links Useful links

There are some great online resources to help improve study habits, including apps like Flora and Cold Turkey to help improve concentration and reduce distractions from the internet.

The Pomodoro Technique is a time management approach that allows you to break down working time into smaller sections, with breaks, to help achieve greater focus and productivity. Likewise, the 'Get Things Done' approach, developed by Dave Allen, helps you to think about your to-do list in terms of projects and tasks, to work to more achievable goals.

For guided meditation techniques and to reduce stress levels, there are websites such as Headspace, Happify and Daylio.

It is helpful to try different techniques until you find one that works best for you.
