As well as the impact on your finances, we know that ready meals are often higher in fat and salt content, and lower in other nutrients. It's also difficult to know the nutritional content of many takeaway choices. When you are living on a student budget, you might find that, once you know how, cooking for yourself can be a cheaper and healthier option.

Feedback:

Cooking for yourself every day may seem overwhelming, but ultimately it allows you to make smarter choices over your portion sizes and ingredients. With a few staple items, you can recreate your favourite takeaway-style meals at home. Batch cooking could save you time and money, and supermarket shortcuts, such as pasta sauces and curry powders, are readily available to help you out.

Simplicity is the key and getting into a routine that works for you is the first step.

If you travel abroad, try to take the opportunity to try new dishes and flavours. You might discover a new favourite dish, and food is a great way to experience new cultures.

A great way to eat more healthily and save money is to prepare and cook your own food, if you can (check the 'Useful links' pod to learn some tips for eating well on a budget). Cooking for yourself means you can be more in control of your choices, for example checking the food miles of the ingredients, buying local or seasonal if possible, or perhaps choosing to consume fewer animal products for environmental reasons.

Preparing your own food also means that you can choose your favourite ingredients and cook them exactly how you like them. Cooking with family, friends or housemates also helps to build your knowledge and skills, increases confidence, and supports positive attitudes and personal relationships (Mills, S. et al., 2017).

I focus on the fresh foods (vegetables, dairy, etc.) I'll need for the next few days and write a shopping list. It's important to me to reduce food waste, so I think about how I can use ingredients in a few different meals. I also keep a good stock of canned, frozen and packaged foods that are low in sugar and salt. These are good to bulk out meals or for days when I can't be bothered to run to the shops.

I never go grocery shopping when I'm hungry! I end up just grabbing things from the shelf I don't need, and it's usually not the healthiest foods. My housemates and I eat together a few times a week, so we'll often go shopping together and share the food bill. This means we can buy in bulk, which is generally cheaper, and we get to practise our cooking and try out new recipes on each other.

When I first started studying here, I found it difficult – moving away from home to another country, starting uni, and looking after myself for the first time in my life! Food has always been a big comfort for me, so I was worried when I went to the supermarket and couldn't find my favourite foods. My friends in the International Society tipped me off about which shops have a good range of international foods, so I stock up on familiar snacks and ingredients whenever I can! I've also got more confident trying out new styles of cooking and enjoy trying out local restaurants with friends.

As well as the social benefits, cooking and eating with others can even provide the opportunity to learn about new cultures by sharing traditional home-cooked dishes.
