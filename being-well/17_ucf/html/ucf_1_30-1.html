There are several potential sources of money to help with your tuition and living costs at university, and you might be entitled to some, or even all of them.

Click on the steps to learn about funding options that are available to students.

Continue on to find out about the different types of loans available.

 Tuition fee loan 1

Eligible Australian students and permanent residents who meet requirements can apply for a HELP loan, which is paid directly to your university. This is paid back once you are earning over a certain threshold.

 Centrelink payments 2

You might be able to get Centrelink student payments if your parents earn below a certain threshold, or you are an independent student. Services Australia will be able to advise on if you are eligible, and help you apply (see 'Useful links' pod).

 Scholarships 3

Eligible undergraduate and postgraduate students, both domestic and international, can receive a scholarship from the Australian Government to help with fees and living costs. An allowance for living costs is usually paid directly to you, and fees paid to the university. These scholarships are merit-based and awarded by individual universities. Universities will also have their own scholarships, and you should check with student support about what is available.

None of these statements are true, but they are common things that you might have heard people say about your HELP loan, as you may see from other people's answers! In reality, the amount of HELP loan you have outstanding doesn't show up anywhere other than in your myGov account, which will show how much you owe and any repayments you have made. Although your HELP loan doesn't show on your credit file, home lenders may treat your repayments like any other liability and take this into account when assessing how much you can borrow. Also, your loan repayments are entirely based on your income, no one else's, so no one else can ever be asked to pay it for you. You do have to pay your loan back if you move overseas though – it's a common myth that you don't, but it's not true. The Australian Tax Office (ATO), which administers repayments, will look at how much you are earning overseas and calculate the minimum repayment threshold.
