There is much written about being 'addicted' to various online activities, and the World Health Organization has now defined online addiction and gaming addiction as recognised mental health conditions. However, these addictions require clinical diagnosis and we are often too quick to claim addiction when what we actually mean is compulsive behaviour. This means persistent or repetitive behaviours that are not necessarily disruptive.

This section considers a number of factors that might suggest signs of addiction but do not, of themselves, mean you are addicted to online activity.

 Your context Your context

If you feel that you would like to talk to someone about the amount of time you spend online, or the way being online makes you feel, consider contacting your university support services. They will be able to give advice and allow you the opportunity to discuss your concerns.

Most mobile devices now provide functionality to allow you to look at your screen time and time spent on different apps to help you manage your time online. Some helpful resources are listed in the 'Useful links' pod, but if your online activities begin to impact your daily life and studies, your university support services or a GP will be able to offer support and advice.
