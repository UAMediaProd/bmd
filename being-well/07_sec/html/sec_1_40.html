Negative Automatic Thoughts (NATs) are unfair critical pieces of self-talk that can influence your thinking without you being aware of them. We all have NATs and recognising the ones that impact you most will help you to limit their influence and start to learn how to challenge them.

 Negative Automatic Thoughts (NATs) All-or-nothing thinking

This simplistic either/or thinking puts you under great pressure as you have only two self-talk messages to choose from, "If I'm not a success, I'm a failure". This can also be called 'black-and-white' or dichotomous thinking.

 Catastrophising

This is when you take one negative incident and apply the worst-case scenario to other areas of your life or future. This cognitive distortion can then be interpreted as if it is fixed in the future, and it can become self-fulfilling, e.g. "My partner has just broken up with me and I'll never find anyone else".

 The use of absolute language

"Should", "shouldn't", "always" and "never" are all examples. You have a rigid idea of how you should behave and are very critical if that does not happen. "I use absolute language all the time and I shouldn't because I must be better than that." See what we did there?

 Discounting the positive

Do you find it hard to accept a compliment? Or to recognise when you have done something well? Do you undermine yourself by finding excuses, e.g. "That exam went well, but that doesn't mean I'm clever – I just got lucky with the questions". Next time someone compliments you, accept it. If that is hard to do, practise!

 False emotional reasoning

You think something must be true because you feel it. For example, "I feel worried about the presentation I have to give, therefore I am not ready to do it and I won't do well".

 Mind reading

This is when you project your own negative self-talk onto others, presuming you have read their mind. By projecting your negative thoughts onto others, you have assumed they think negatively of you, too.

 Overgeneralisation

Overgeneralising involves making a sweeping negative conclusion that goes far beyond the current situation. For example, "I felt uncomfortable with Charlie on that first meet and greet night for residential students. I don't think I fit into this university". It is closely linked to catastrophising.

 Personalisation

You believe others are behaving negatively because of you, without considering other possible explanations for their behaviour. For example, "Gina was really short with me in the kitchen this morning. What have I done to upset her?"

 Peer support Peer support

Active listening is the key to supporting your friends in recognising and challenging their Negative Automatic Thoughts (NATs).

It is tempting to say, "Yeah me too" and talk about yourself, and it is very easy to move straight to problem-solving mode.

It is much more helpful to reflect on what your friend has said so they feel understood. Try paraphrasing what they have said and conclude with a question, for example, "Have I understood that properly?". This allows them to actually hear their negative self-talk spoken out loud and gives them the opportunity to correct it.

You may have recognised a few of these NATs in your own thoughts and behaviours. Training yourself to recognise NATs can be highly beneficial for your sense of self-confidence and wellbeing.

You can learn more about these kinds of thinking traps by doing an online cognitive behavioural therapy course. There are a number of these in Australia, including MindSpot, Mental Health Online and This Way Up.

Recognising Negative Automatic Thoughts does not happen overnight. It takes a great deal of work and some people will benefit from professional help. Once you have recognised your NATs, the process usually goes like this:

  • You recognise that your NATs took hold after the event. Don't let a NAT make you think you cannot overcome your NATs (think about it!)
  • You recognise your NATs taking hold during a situation. Sometimes you can challenge these in the moment and respond accordingly, but often you will not. Recognise that this is progress
  • After a while, as you get better at noticing your NATs, you either challenge them or let them go, rather than acting on them
  • As you get better at managing your NATs, you will stop being aware you are doing it. You will have a new way of thinking.
