Establishing a good study-life balance is part of appreciating yourself not just as a 'student', but as a whole person. This module focuses particularly on managing time, getting enough sleep and investing in relationships. These are all part of looking after your mind and your body; keeping these in good shape is core to doing well at your studies. See this as your long-term strategy to support your success as a student.

Managing your time at university can feel different to anything you have done before, with most of it down to you to juggle and plan. It can be easy to imagine you can get everything done without ever mapping out your expectations of yourself, so a good starting point is to consider how you already spend your time at university or, if you have not started yet, how you imagine spending it.

Feedback

Academic work: This total will vary depending on your level of study, but if you are a full-time student, it is a good rule of thumb to treat studying as a full-time job. This includes taught time like lectures and seminars, as well as personal study, which you must organise yourself. Tutors can give good guidance on what is needed.

Paid work: Students are generally recommended to do no more than 15-20 hours per week of paid work, although everyone's needs and availability will be different. International students must check for any work restrictions on their visas. Paid work can provide good experience for later employment.

Cooking and eating: Make time to cook and eat properly at home if possible – it's cheaper, and a good way to socialise with housemates if you have them. Make it a proper break – your brain will work better afterwards. And stay hydrated!

Sleeping: Sleep is food for the brain, and you should aim for eight hours of rest a night. See the screen titled: Why sleep matters for advice on improving your sleep quality.

Travelling: Sometimes this can be work time too, if you are on public transport. Or you can use it to relax – have something enjoyable to listen to or a book to read to help you switch off.

Chores: Keeping up with these saves time (and often money) in the long-term, and they are easier to manage if done regularly. Plus it will make you popular if you live in shared accommodation!

Family commitments: These will depend on your particular circumstances, but it is important to schedule enough time to socialise and communicate with loved ones so you can stay connected.

Voluntary work: This can be fulfilling and fun; it can take you outside of your subject area, enhance your employability skills, and help you to make new social connections.

Exercise/keeping fit: This can often double up with social time (e.g. going to sports clubs or going running with friends). It's good for your health and is a great stress reliever.

Socialising: However you do this, spending enjoyable time with other people is important and can have a positive effect on your general sense of wellbeing.

Relaxing: By yourself or with others, this is as important as anything else – your mind and body need time to 'switch off', de-stress and recuperate, to be ready for the next day.

Perhaps the time calculator activity gave you some food for thought. Don't worry if you can't always stick to a schedule – each week can be different; you can't plan for everything that life may bring.

 Time management This means getting the most out of the time you have. Everyone does this differently, but there are some core things to think about. A good starting point is to begin an activity by asking, "Is this worth my time?" and to finish it with, "Am I glad I did that?" Know yourself Are you a morning person or an evening person? Do you work best alone or in a group? Do you need to be alone or with others to relax and refuel? It's important to know what works for you, and to work with, not against, yourself. If you focus better in the evenings, then use that time for the difficult tasks and do more routine things in the morning – or vice versa, if you spring out of bed ready to do your best. Have a daily/weekly routine Students often set their own schedules around the lectures, seminars and tutorials that are part of their course. Having a routine saves a lot of time otherwise spent thinking about what to do. The routine can take account of when you are most alert and focused – don't choose those times to do your laundry! End each day knowing what you plan to do tomorrow – write yourself a list before you stop for the day. This saves time having to remember where you were up to later. Prioritise Priorities change over time, so take a look at your priorities every week to make sure they are up to date. Check whether they are important, urgent or both – the latter goes to the top of the list. Priorities need to take in commitments to family, friends, paid work and volunteering as well as study. As well as what needs doing, think about how you are going to do it – this will give you a better idea of the time required. Use lists – priorities need to be written down, not just be in your head. Use a time planner University deadlines often come in a bunch, and everyone else will be looking for the same library books or lab space at the same time as you. Planning ahead is the only way around this – check the time planner in the 'Download' pod to get started. Always allow time for things to go wrong – no schedule will work perfectly! If you fall behind your schedule, make the time up as soon as possible – it becomes harder the longer you leave it. And include time to relax! Time is finite We can have this internal picture of a perfect time manager who can always do everything. This can relate to perfectionism, when nothing feels good enough to hand in. But good time management means knowing that time is finite, and making conscious choices about how you are going to use it; what you are and are not going to do. It does not mean being ruthless with yourself or making unreasonable demands on yourself or others. Time is like money in the bank, only without the overdraft facility – you can't borrow forward! Personal fulfilment Personal fulfilment is just as important as academic success. Being at university offers many activities and opportunities outside of your course that are just as much part of being a student, and all of it contributes to developing you as a whole person. Making time to keep yourself in good emotional and physical shape will mean that you work and study better too.

We can't make more time, but we can all make better use of the time that we have. Balance is important – time to work, time to rest and time to have fun are all part of being well, staying well and succeeding as a student.

 Peer support Peer support

You can support your friends by accepting their way of managing time and not calling them out as anti-social or 'killjoys' just because they don't want to do things when you want to do them. Keep them included in invitations; just because someone says no to a movie night once because of a deadline doesn't mean they wouldn't be pleased to be asked the next time.
