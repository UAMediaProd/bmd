Alongside doing my PhD in Medieval Literature, I care for my elderly mother who has dementia. I find it really challenging to manage my time, as I need to do archival research which takes me away from her for several days at a time. I am able to arrange for professional carers to come in on a daily basis, which makes things a bit easier, but I also feel hugely guilty for being away from her at a time when she needs me so much. I feel like I never know whether I'm a daughter, a nurse or a student at any one time!

I'm really lucky, as fairly early on in my studies I was able to get a paid position as a tutor in my department. I get to do a lot of demonstrating to undergraduate students in the lab and I've also delivered a couple of lectures on my research project. The problem is that I don't know if I'm a member of staff or a student any more! The undergraduates all see me as a member of staff and queue up outside my lab to talk to me about their problems. It's taking up a lot of my time and I never seem to get dedicated time to spend on doing the experiments I should be doing! But I do need the money and, as I want a career as an academic, it is really important for me to be getting a lot of teaching experience.

I did my undergraduate degree and Master's at the same institution, and was really fortunate to have a lot of support while I was there. My tutors really pushed me to do my best and they really believed in my potential. They were the ones who encouraged me to apply for this PhD studentship at a big prestigious university but now that I'm here, I'm not sure it was the right move at all! I feel like such a fish out of water – just down to the small things like finding out where the toilets are or what time the library is open – I just took all of that for granted at my previous university but now I'm starting from scratch in so many ways. I'm also so worried that I'm really not good enough to belong here. I'm not finding this transition easy at all!

Charlotte just needs to knuckle down and get her assignments done on time – even if that means ignoring her work commitments

With growing numbers of international students, and those taking non-traditional pathways into higher education, it is becoming increasingly common for postgraduate research students to be working or caring for family members whilst undertaking their studies. Not only does this make it harder to achieve a good work-life balance but there is also role identity conflict and a feeling of 'not belonging' to the traditional concept of what a postgraduate researcher looks like.
