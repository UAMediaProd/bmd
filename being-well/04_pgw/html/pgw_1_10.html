Welcome to this module on postgraduate research student wellbeing. Doing postgraduate research is a wonderful opportunity to study a subject you love in depth, meet other people doing the same thing and produce work that contributes to the existing body of knowledge. It is completely normal, given these opportunities and responsibilities, to sometimes feel overwhelmed and to have doubts along your academic journey. It can be a time of great stress, and this topic helps you to identify the issues or concerns you may currently have with regards to your own wellbeing. The materials will also help you to explore specific tools to improve your own wellbeing while studying for a Master's or PhD.

Online communities such as the Black Dog Institute and Beyond Blue offer support for those experiencing anxiety or depression.

Try to think about how your research project might be affecting your mood, and make some plans for the journey still ahead. It is very common for postgraduate research students to find that their studies involve a rollercoaster of emotions as they progress, and many specifically encounter 'mid-point blues' and feel very demotivated after the initial excitement of starting their postgraduate studies has worn off.

Feedback:

If you find you are having significant and sustained dips in mood then please do seek professional help from your GP or university support services. Online services such as the Black Dog Institute can also be really helpful (see the 'Useful links' pod).

Being a postgraduate research student can be unexpectedly more challenging than being an undergraduate, and it is certainly far from a straightforward journey! However, this doesn't mean that you can't be in control of your health and wellbeing. You've just taken the first, important steps to identify any areas that you need to focus on in order to bring balance to your life.

I really enjoyed my time as a PhD student – it was the first opportunity I'd ever had to focus on a piece of research that I was passionate about and really wanted to do. That said, it was pretty challenging learning how to juggle a massive project and all the other aspects of life! I reached out to other PhD students early on in my degree and found them a really good source of information and support – particularly those who were further on than me.

Patrick, 27

I was incredibly excited to start my PhD in Biochemistry. I really thought that I was going to change the world! I had sponsorship from a charity but there was a lot of pressure to finish within three years, which I found tough. The worst part, though, was midway through, when my results were not showing what I wanted them to and I felt like I wasn't going anywhere. Apparently it's really common to feel like this at the mid-point but no one told me until I was past it! I found it really helpful to meet with my fellow PhD students in the bar every Wednesday evening and have a bit of a whinge. I don't know what I would have done without them!

Fazar, 36
