While studying, you may be commuting daily from an established home, establishing a new permanent home, or moving into a new temporary home at the start of each academic year. Either way, it is worthwhile thinking about how to establish the best possible 'home base' for whatever stage you are at on your journey, in your personal space and on campus.

A good home base starts with your own personal space – making sure it's a welcoming place where you can feel safe, be yourself, rest and recharge. If you are comfortable studying at your university, try to find multiple spaces on campus where you can work between or after your scheduled classes, as well as those for relaxation and breaks from study.

Personal space

Comfort objects

Moving out of home can be challenging at times. A favourite mascot from childhood, or a familiar pillow or bed throw, is a classic way to feel more comfortable in an unfamiliar environment. Having a favourite book to dip into or your musical instrument to play can also be a great way to comfort yourself in a difficult moment.

Valued connections

Decorate your room with pictures and objects that connect you with the things that are important to you. Use photo collages to keep family, friends and good times in mind. Keep a thread of connection between who you were and who you are becoming.

Regular rest

Getting into a good sleep routine can make a huge difference to how well you manage challenges and stress. If you are sensitive to noise, some earplugs or soothing music might help you get to sleep when your housemates are still up. See the module titled: Study-life balance for more ideas on getting good sleep.

Contact with nature

Contact with nature has a calming effect, and the responsibility of caring for a plant can be a useful distraction during difficult times. Also, try to get outdoors regularly and preferably in touch with nature – perhaps you could find a scenic route to walk to your classes sometimes? See the module titled: Stress, anxiety and depression for more information about the benefits of spending time outside.

Exercise

Getting regular exercise is one of the most important things you can do to maintain positive health and wellbeing. Try to set up a basic daily exercise routine, like using a smartwatch or app to set yourself a target number of steps to walk each day. See the module titled: Being active for more ideas on how to build activity into your daily life.

Dedicated study area

If you need to use your bedroom as a study base then set up a desk or separate area for study. Keeping your bed as a study-free zone helps maintain the psychological distance needed for good sleep and relaxation routines. Consider setting up a study base on campus as your main study zone, especially if you struggle to switch off during intense periods of study.

Keeping in touch

Set up a routine to touch base with friends on a regular basis and to make time for family if you live at home. It's a great idea to try to talk with your friends outside of university study (not just through scrolling through their updates on social media), so you know how they are really doing – and can share your own experiences with them as well.

Shared routines

If you are living in your family home, it can be useful to be clear about your responsibilities and how much you are able to contribute to the housework. When living out of home, you won't always become best friends with your housemates, but it can really help to get together at the start and divide the daily tasks or at least make a cleaning roster. This helps manage common sources of conflict.

Familiar food

Access to familiar foods is particularly important if you are living out of home or in a new space – even just a few items for special occasions can be a comfort. Learn how to replicate your favourite meals, and consider cooking with housemates occasionally so you can share favourites with others.

Favourite music

Make playlists that remind you of good times in the past, and songs that help to lift your mood. You'll make new memories and new playlists, but old playlists help remind you of who you are and what you like.

Campus study base

Course handbook

Make full use of your course handbook and all the induction and familiarisation activities at the start of each year, so you are really clear about what is expected and what resources are available to support your study. Check the online learning platform to access all the course materials. If things still aren't clear, ask!

Kit bag

Make sure you've got a bag that can fit everything you need for the day, so you can set up camp in your usual spot or go somewhere a bit different if you need to. Pack snacks and items (like a book to read) to help you take regular breaks.

Campus study base

Even if your bedroom is well set up for studying, it can be useful to have a 'change of scenery' and study somewhere different occasionally. Choose the space to suit your study preferences, or have more than one space to suit different needs, such as a quiet space if you are sensitive to noise, and a favourite meeting place for discussions, study groups or socialising. This is especially important if the time it takes to travel to university might make it more difficult to meet with other students. Check whether your university offers dedicated study areas for students with additional requirements, e.g. students with disabilities, or mature or postgraduate students.

Personalised timetable

Once you get your formal class timetable and contact hours, fill in a personalised timetable of additional independent study hours so that you build up a familiar daily work routine. This is easier if you have a regular study space and don't have to think about where to go or make new plans every time. (See the module titled: Study-life balance for more ideas on setting up a routine.)

Filing system

Set up a consistent filing system for your notes and assignments, so you can find things later. And always make back-ups of your work, so you don't have to worry about losing it.

Study habits

Get advice from study skills tutors about getting into good study habits, and about the tech to help you with this. There are lots of excellent apps and programs to help with recordings and note-taking during lectures. (See the module titled: Studying well for more study tips.)

Study partners

You will likely be given a group assignment at some point in the course, but it can be very helpful to form separate study groups to offer mutual support with getting into study routines. See if anyone on your course wants to arrange a regular meet-up to study together – this is also a good way to extend your friendship network.

Regular meals

Make sure you plan for eating an energy-boosting breakfast and lunch every day – and drinking plenty of water – to keep up your concentration and motivation levels. Plan ahead and, if possible, make packed lunches to keep costs down and nutrition levels high. (See the module titled: Eating well for more tips and ideas.)

Active lifestyle

Taking a short study break for physical activity can help you release study stress and refocus. There are lots of activities you can do to relieve the mental loads of study, such as taking a walk, playing a game of soccer with friends or doing some yoga. You will be able to concentrate more effectively and get more work done if you give yourself breaks to release stress in the body and active thoughts in your head. This is especially important with exam or assessment deadlines. (See the module titled: Being active for more ideas for how to build exercise into your routine.)

Familiar surroundings

You will get to know the buildings, lecture theatres and seminar rooms where you have your timetabled classes, but make an effort to explore more widely and get familiar with other buildings to feel even more at home on campus and find different places for taking a break and hanging out with others

As you can see from some of the suggestions on this screen, a 'home base' is not just about familiar places, but also about the emotional security that comes from familiar routines and supportive relationships. It's important to create and maintain a good home base by keeping in touch with our existing sources of security and maintaining helpful routines while we start to build new sources of familiarity, support and connection.

What are your next steps for improving your university home base?

Setting up a good home base is like laying a solid foundation for a building. When you meet challenges and things feel a bit shaky, check your foundations and think about what you can do to strengthen them. These ideas will apply as you move on from university and establish a home base for your working life.
