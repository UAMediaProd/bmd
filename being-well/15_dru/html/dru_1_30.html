It's not a good idea to mix stimulants and empathogens with alcohol.

True False

Mixing stimulants and empathogens with alcohol

Correct. There are many reasons why mixing alcohol with stimulant and empathogen drugs is a bad idea. Stimulants make you feel euphoric, energetic and overconfident. In this state, people are more likely to drink more alcohol and can therefore overdose more easily. Additionally, the most common stimulant – cocaine – combines with alcohol in the body to form a compound that is damaging to the liver. When alcohol is combined with empathogens, the 'high' is reduced. This is very misleading, and people may take more empathogen drugs than they should, increasing the chances of overdose or bad experiences.

Stimulants and empathogens

If my friend is feeling short of breath and anxious after taking either a stimulant or an empathogen drug, I should try to talk at the same speed as them and let them sit in a hunched position.

True False

Helping a friend

Incorrect. Someone that has taken too much of a stimulant or empathogen drug might:

  • Have dilated pupils
  • Be short of breath
  • Experience chest pains or palpitations
  • Be irritable, talkative or anxious.

If a friend has taken too much of any of these substances, take them to a quiet and calm space. Encourage them to sit up straight and open their chest – a hunched position will make the symptoms worse. Try to talk to them in a low and calm voice. The key is to slow them down both mentally and physically. If you have strong concerns about your friend's condition, seek professional help sooner rather than later. You won't get into trouble for seeking medical help even though drugs are involved.

The effects and dependence are similar when smoking cannabis with tobacco, or without.

True False

Mixing with tobacco

Correct. Mixing tobacco and cannabis is a bad combination. Tobacco is highly addictive, and if both are combined the chances of developing dependency to cannabis are much higher than when cannabis is consumed on its own. Additionally, cannabis and tobacco are often smoked together in a joint (rolled cigarette), which means that you add all the additional health risks associated with smoking.

Cannabinoids

Cannabis is harmless.

True False

No drugs are harmless

Incorrect. Many cannabis users have the misconception that cannabis is harmless and that they can easily manage their habits. Cannabis does have negative effects on your body and mind. For instance, cannabis interacts with the system that develops and matures the brain, which can reduce or impair cognitive abilities in teenagers. Cannabis alters the quality of sleep, which can have a profound negative impact on mood and concentration. It may also impact short-term memory in users of all ages. Regular use of cannabis may trigger anxiety attacks and psychotic episodes in people with predisposition to these.

Psychedelics

If a friend is having a bad experience, remind them that they are hallucinating.

True False

Looking after your friends

Incorrect. Someone that has taken too much of a psychedelic substance will probably make little sense, hallucinate and have vacant eyes. Never tell someone under the influence of psychedelics that they are hallucinating. This will make them feel even worse, leading to anxiety. Try to follow their thought process. They are in a very vulnerable state, so it is easy to influence their thinking and emotions. For example, if they think someone is following them, tell them that you know a safe place to hide and that everything is going to be OK. Bring them to a safe, quiet space and stay with them until the effects wear off.

Dissociatives and depressants

These drugs are safe to combine with each other as well as others.

True False

Avoid mixing

Incorrect. As we learnt earlier, depressants are drugs that reduce stimulation and arousal, whereas dissociatives produce the feeling that the mind detaches from the body and the environment. Both of these drugs combine quite badly with any substance, but it can be extremely unsafe to mix them. Depressant plus depressant is dangerous as it can slow down your brain and body too much, resulting in fainting or even coma. Dissociative plus dissociative can have a similar effect and makes accidents more likely due to reduced coordination.

If a friend needs to be taken to the hospital because they have passed out after taking any of these substances, don't tell the medical professionals about all the substances that your friend has consumed.

True False

Patient confidentiality

Incorrect. In Australia, all patients have the right to a confidential medical service no matter what type of drug has been consumed. When seeking help, it's important to give as much information as possible. This will help professionals to act quickly and reduce the chances of major harm. Helpful information includes the amount taken, the colour and shape of the drug, and where it was obtained. The ambulance office, doctor or hospital will not call the police just because someone has taken an illicit drug, unless they are violent or committing some other crime. Medical professionals may feel obliged to disclose the information if there is a major incident, such as a death.

 Peer support Peer support

If you are concerned that your friend is having a bad experience with drugs, the most important thing is to seek medical attention. However, there are some practical things you can do to help them:

  • If they are on stimulants or empathogens, bring them to a quiet and calm place, and slow them down physically and mentally
  • If they are on psychedelics, never tell them they are hallucinating. Follow what they are saying and bring them to a quiet and safe space, and stay with them
  • If they are on dissociatives or depressants, keep them awake and in a comfortable position, so they don't accidently hurt themselves.
