 Financial support HELP loans

Most domestic students will apply for government funding to cover fees.

 Centrelink payments

Undergraduate and some postgraduate students may be eligible for Centrelink student payments, rent assistance and Child Care Subsidy (CCS).

 University bursaries and scholarships

Check with your university about what they offer in additional financial support. If you are eligible for a bursary or scholarship, do you need to apply for it? When will you receive this support, and how will it be paid?

 Funding from other sources

Many charities, trusts and other organisations have funds for students in education. Your local community may have funds available, so check what is possible. International students can access scholarships from their home nation, as well as Australia. If you are employed, make sure you ask your employer about sponsorship schemes.

 Help from people you know and savings

You may be lucky enough to be able to rely on help from others, but check how much this will be, and which costs you are going to have to cover yourself. If you can save some money before you start university, this can be helpful. If you already have savings, are you willing to tap into these if the going gets tough?

 Making money whilst studying

Working part-time, selling items like old tech or clothes, babysitting, taking online surveys, and blogging – these are all things that students have done in order to supplement their income. Think about things that could enhance your CV as well as your bank balance, but always put your wellbeing first.

Imagine that you have worked out how much funding you have through Centrelink, bursaries and scholarships and it's not enough to cover your costs. How could you try to make up the shortfall?

Think about what you can do before you embark on your studies as well as during your studies. Do you have savings? Can you work and save before you start your course? Can anyone loan you the money? Will you need to work alongside studying?

First, revisit your budget and check for any areas where you can make further savings. Can you choose cheaper accommodation, for example? Can you use public transport rather than a car? Most students work alongside study, or work over the long vacation periods. Start applying for jobs as soon as you have a firm place at university and seek advice from your university's career service or student association. If you already have a job, can you change your hours to allow you to continue to work, or transfer to a branch near campus?

For some students, it is a necessity to work alongside studying. Can you balance work, study, and your financial and general wellbeing?

How easy is it to get a job in the area of your chosen university? How many hours can you realistically work without compromising the standard of your academic work and your wellbeing? Can you be creative about ways to make money?

If you need a part-time job, start researching options as soon as possible and polish up your CV. If you are an international student, check that your visa allows you to work. Think carefully about whether the job will work alongside lectures, seminars and self-directed study. If regular part-time work isn't practical, then casual or seasonal work at local festivals and events may be available, or your university may employ students for Open Day, ambassador, or residential college schemes. Be aware of your employee rights and question employment that is potentially exploitative or unsafe. The careers service at your university will be able to give you guidance, and student services can support you if you are feeling vulnerable or exploited – never be afraid to seek support.

What else can you do to make your income and expenditure balance up?

Think outside of the box! What are your talents, and how can you use them to make money? For example, perhaps you can sing, dance, write poetry, care for children or pets, do make-up, or design a website.

Students have come up with all sorts of ways to make money using their talents. Often their efforts to make money have enhanced their CVs, too. You can also earn 'stuff' – such as free samples, vouchers and trial packs – rather than cash, by completing online surveys or writing online product reviews. It may be tempting to make money from work that is potentially exploitative, as the financial rewards can appear high. Always seek advice and support if you are not sure, and put your safety and wellbeing first.

You can just about cover everything if you keep to a tight budget and work alongside study, but what happens if you lose your job or something unexpected happens?

Do you have any funds to fall back on in an emergency, or someone who could help if the need arose? Will you need an overdraft facility on your bank account? Does your university offer any financial assistance?

Life has a habit of moving in ways we don't expect, so having a fallback plan is helpful. If you can see that finances are going to be difficult, speak to your university about scholarships and grants, and who is eligible to apply. Don't be afraid to ask for budgeting and money advice from student services.

Whichever way you look at things, you can't seem to make your budget work. What are the options?

Are there alternative study options that could be easier on your finances? Would delaying your studies mean that you have time to save up?

Sometimes, despite best laid plans, the sums just don't work. Financial stress can have a major effect on wellbeing, so consider study options carefully. Student advisors will be able to support you with your decision and you should also access wellbeing services if you feel that you are struggling. Distance or part-time study is often more manageable. Deferring study to work and save is a good option for many. Talk to your university about whether you can defer or switch your mode of study. If you are already studying and you are not managing well financially, then you may wish to talk to an advisor about your study options to ease the stress.
