As we have discovered, eating healthily is important. However, healthy attitudes to eating are important as well. The term 'healthy attitudes' means that eating and food are just one part of life. Food is not an obsession or something that worries you all the time. It means that:

  • You treat food and eating as something enjoyable
  • Your food intake varies, and you may eat less on some days and more on others
  • You don't have strict rules or judgements around food or cut out foods or food groups (unless for a specific reason, e.g. intolerance, religious, cultural or ethical reasons)
  • You don't track your food intake obsessively
  • You don't diet in a way that is compulsive or regularly skip meals
  • You enjoy some foods more than others just because you prefer the taste.

Thinking that you, a friend or a loved one may have serious issues with food, or disordered eating (e.g. restricting food intake, regularly overeating, having unrealistic or obsessive food rules), is challenging. However, it is important to discuss your concerns with someone you trust. If you are unsure who to turn to, your GP is a good place to start. There are also many online or telephone support services available, if this feels more comfortable. Talking about these issues is a first great step in the right direction (see the 'Your context' pod for details). Help and support are available to you, no matter what you are going through.
