Drug laws change constantly, so it is good practise to check your state or territory's drug laws if you plan to try a psychoactive drug, or to check the laws ahead of time if you are travelling to a different country. It is important to note that some things that aren't viewed as serious in Australia might carry significantly higher penalties abroad. Remember that, in most countries, being under the influence of drugs is not a criminal offence.

In Australia, most drug laws are state- or territory-based. There are also some drug-related laws that are made by the Australian government (such as those related to importing drugs). The legislative document that determines how a substance is classified in Australia is called The Poisons Standard. It categorises drugs into 'schedules' that determine when or how they can be distributed. Drugs that are criminalised are also listed under separate criminal drug legislation. Schedule 9 drugs are prohibited in Australia, except for some research purposes. They include heroin, MDMA and LSD. Schedule 8 drugs are controlled drugs. These are considered to have a therapeutic use but high abuse potential.

Being caught using, supplying or in possession of drugs at university could also result in disciplinary action, and have far-reaching consequences.

Remember that a drug's legal status does not tell you anything about how harmful it is going to be for you. What matters the most is how you consume it. Always follow harm reduction strategies to reduce the risk.
