There will be times where you find yourself supporting someone else who is lacking confidence, whose self-esteem is low and who doesn't believe they can be resilient. This section will help you think about how you can help them. It will also help you think about your own self-talk and the difference between how compassionate you are to others compared to how hard you are on yourself.

Minimising the issue

Lucy is clearly upset but this response dismisses her feelings by minimising the issue. You might respond like this because you want to give Lucy some rational advice – "Just ignore them" – but ignoring negative comments or not caring what others think is very hard in reality. This advice will likely make Lucy feel worse.

Active listening

Properly listening to someone involves acknowledging them and reflecting back what they have said to check you have understood correctly. Asking what, or perhaps who, has helped before is a great question to ask as almost always people have found their own solutions to challenging situations before.

How someone else feels is different to how you felt

It can be easy to fall into the trap of thinking how you felt and acted in a similar situation is the same as how your friend currently feels. By instantly explaining how things were for you, your attention is off how Lucy feels now and she will recognise that. Letting others know what they are experiencing is not unusual is fine, but spend time understanding what Lucy is feeling.

Blaming

Accepting Priscilla's statement that she has failed will only strengthen her negative self-talk. It can be useful to find out whether the steps Priscilla had taken in attempting her assignment were sufficient, but the way this is phrased sounds accusatory. This response will likely make Priscilla feel worse.

A similar experience is not the same experience

Normalising that all students have a bump in the road in their degree is often helpful as people tend to think they are the only ones to struggle. Knowing others experience similar situations helps people to feel that their response is normal. What is not helpful here is assuming that your experience is the same as Priscilla's, when you actually received different grades.

Learning opportunity

Acknowledging how Priscilla is feeling is a good start here, as it helps Priscilla to know that she has been heard. The response quickly helps Priscilla shift from her all-or-nothing fixed-mindset thinking to seeing how this is an excellent opportunity to learn and improve. Almost certainly, there will be positive comments in the feedback, as well as constructive criticism she can learn from. This response isn't about you but is all about Priscilla's next steps.

Someone else's fault

It can be very tempting to absolve your friend from any responsibility and blame other people. Blaming others is easy to do but any relationship involves two people who are jointly responsible for making it work. The implicit criticism of Mohammed in this response is that he fell in love with a 'manipulative' person and he shouldn't have. Mohammed's negative self-talk is likely to pick up on this and increase his self-blame.

Praise is not always helpful

Your response here is to praise Mohammed's strengths and indeed that can be very helpful, but it can also be counterproductive. If Mohammed is such a great catch, as you have just told him, he may feel self-blame that he still couldn't keep the person he loves. This response does not leave Mohammed any room to learn and grow as it is not helping him to reflect on what has been happening in the relationship.

Compassionate acknowledgement of the pain

It is so tempting to offer advice and you so want to find the right phrase to remove the pain from your friend, but no one can. This response is all about acknowledging that it is a painful process and one that will take a while for Mohammed to emerge from. You offer some specific actions you can do together to help him; reiterate he has a good support network and encourage him to think of previous difficult times he has overcome.

This response is one designed to learn about gossip. You have not enquired as to how Erica is feeling or if she needs any support, and it also shows a lack of empathy about a serious situation. Erica is likely to feel dismissed and alone by this response.

It is normal in a traumatic situation to think back to whether you could have done more to help or prevent the situation. Negative self-talk is very good at creating self-reproach and you insinuating Erica has not done enough will only feed her critical inner voice. Putting your own (positive) experience into the conversation will only make Erica feel worse, although you may feel momentarily superior.

You have also sensibly recognised the limits of the help you can offer in this area by signposting Erica to some of the support available at the university, while also offering a listening ear yourself.

 Peer support Peer support

Active listening involves giving your total attention to the other person, so make sure you are away from distractions.

Avoid jumping to problem solving or sharing your own experience and concentrate on really understanding how the other person feels. Reflect back what you have heard using their own words as much as possible – for example, "OK, what I think you are telling me is that you don't feel good enough to stay on that society committee. Is that right?". This both gives the speaker a chance to hear what they have been thinking out loud and allows them the opportunity to correct you. Use open questions, such as "Can you tell me more about why you think that?". Open questions engage the speaker more.

Remember that people communicate by more than words alone. A person may say, "I feel great" but their body language and tone are telling you something else. Ensure you pick up on that discrepancy by saying, "I hear that you are saying you feel great, but the way you are saying it makes me think you are not feeling that great. Can you tell me what's really going on for you?".

You should use closed questions to check your understanding and towards the end of any conversation when the speaker has come up with some actions, for example: "You've agreed to give the next committee meeting a go and I've agreed we will meet for coffee beforehand and I will help you manage your worries about it. Does that sound like something you can do?".
