At a time of crisis, there are essentially two ways of finding support: our own internal self-care strategies (things we can do to support ourselves), and external support strategies (people and agencies who can help us cope). With the help of self-care strategies and our external support system, we can develop a safety plan to use generally at times of stress, but specifically at crisis points.

 Exercise to feel good Doing exercise is not only good for us physically, but it also releases endorphins, which are sometimes known as 'natural mood lifters'. This can help to break a cycle of self-harm or reduce suicidal thoughts. Even gentle exercise is beneficial when you are feeling low. STOPP technique

A really useful self-help approach to structure our thinking in response to particular situations is the STOPP technique:

  • S: STOP – Pause for a moment
  • T: TAKE A BREATH – Pay attention to your breathing
  • O: OBSERVE – Notice the types of thoughts you are having or any other sensations you feel
  • P: PERSPECTIVE – Think how might you support yourself right now, e.g. think what advice would you give to a friend
  • P: PRACTISE – Use self-care strategies and review how they work for you.
 Be kind to yourself Taking a deliberate approach to speaking kindly and compassionately to ourselves in times of difficulty. We are probably too quick to self-criticise, whereas repeating self-supporting phrases – as we might encourage a friend in crisis – can really help. Supportive phrases could include: "I'm sorry it's so tough for you right now", and it might help to gently stroke your arms, or hold a soothing soft object such as a pillow. Distraction techniques Distraction can be an important way of supporting ourselves. This is when you do something to take your attention away from difficult thoughts or feelings, such as carrying out an activity or watching a film. If used all the time, of course, it can contribute to ongoing problems, as we never allow ourselves to deal with how we feel. Used carefully, however, distraction can be a useful short-term support strategy. Practise mindfulness Mindfulness strategies can help us focus on the here and now rather than getting hooked on to negative or upsetting thinking, which are often associated with suicidal thinking or self-harm. This can include journaling, swimming or practising yoga. See the module titled: Confidence, self-esteem and resilience for more information on overcoming negative thoughts. Seeking support Telling ourselves that we have to do everything on our own is both unrealistic and unreasonable. We all need the help of others on occasion. Perhaps make a commitment to yourself that you will give talking to someone else a try and see what it is like. Your context Your context

How easy was it to decide on strategies that might work for you? Is there a pattern between those strategies that immediately appeal to you, and those you are less likely to use, such as doing vs. thinking strategies? It can be hard to 'get going' with self-support strategies because, when you are feeling really low, finding the energy to do so is difficult. Once the first step is made, however, it is amazing how quickly some momentum can build. Repeating strategies, and trying out new ones, will become easier over time.

If you are experiencing suicidal thoughts, please contact one of the crisis support services that are available 24/7:

  • Contact Lifeline online or by calling 13 11 14
  • Contact the Suicide Call Back Service online or by calling 1300 659 467

Also, please consider using a suicide safety planning app:

  • Beyond Now
  • ReMinder

Your university might also have an out-of-hours crisis support line. Check with the health or counselling services at your university to find out.
