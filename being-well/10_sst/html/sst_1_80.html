A safety plan can be invaluable not only at times of crisis (when it is often harder to bring positive things to mind), but also as a day-to-day strategy for self-support. Like most things in life, prevention is better than cure and if you are able to develop positive mental health routines, you are less likely to encounter major problems. Or, if you do (as any of us can), you will have the resources and perhaps the resilience to find a less painful way through.

Make a list of the internal and external support strategies you might include in your plan. A good hint is to be as specific as possible. For example, rather than "Ring a helpline", you could write "Ring Lifeline on 13 11 14". This means at times of difficulty you already have all the information you need. If you need to search for it, you are less likely to do it.

If you are experiencing suicidal thoughts, please contact one of the crisis support services that are available 24/7:

  • Contact Lifeline online or by calling 13 11 14
  • Contact the Suicide Call Back Service online or by calling 1300 659 467

Also, please consider using a suicide safety planning app:

  • Beyond Now
  • ReMinder

Your university might also have an out-of-hours crisis support line. Check with the health or counselling services at your university to find out.

This is your starting point for a safety plan. The most effective safety plans are the ones developed over time, and in response to your unique experiences. This can then be an important contributing factor to maintaining your mental health in the best shape, so check back and update the plan as regularly as you need to.
