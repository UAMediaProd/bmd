Humans are emotional beings. We will all feel (and behave) in a variety of ways throughout our lives, depending on what is happening to us at the time.

Emotions are a normal part of life, but sometimes they can be difficult to manage and we may need help to cope with them. We may also need support with some of the unhelpful behaviour patterns that we can fall into when we are stressed, anxious or depressed.

 Your context Your context

When we say 'ask for support', this can mean a few things, depending on your situation. It might mean talking to a friend, family member or university counsellor. If you feel isolated or lonely, it may mean looking at what clubs and groups operate at your university and joining one of them. The idea is to simply reach out and make a connection with another person who can help you take the next step.

Stress and worry

Stress is a common part of life at university. Students experience pressure from deadlines, exam revision, and the need to be creative or innovative. This is all a normal part of getting a qualification. But if you notice that the stress is overwhelming, and not just concentrated at certain times (like before important deadlines or exams), then it might be a good idea to talk to someone. Feeling under pressure before a presentation is understandable. Feeling under pressure relentlessly can be very hard to cope with alone.

Anxiety

Beyond the feeling of pressure comes worrying or feeling 'on edge'. You might overthink situations, going over what you said or did (or didn't say or do). You ruminate or agonise about everything, or about things like social gatherings or your health. It's OK to feel anxious about the big things in life, like an interview or an exam. But if these feelings persist, then it's time to ask for help from someone you trust.

Panic

Panic is a feeling of sudden and overwhelming anxiety or worry. You may be overcome with anxious thoughts, with feelings that something terrible is about to happen. If it happens repeatedly (often in the absence of actual dangers or threats) we might call it a 'panic attack'. This can be very frightening. You may feel physical sensations like your heart racing and your breathing becoming rapid. You may sweat, shake or cry. Don't ignore these feelings, as they are not part of life's normal ups and downs. Ask for help from a wellbeing professional.

Repetitive thoughts or behaviours

When some people feel worried they think the same thing over and over again, or repeat behaviours (e.g. checking that doors are locked or that appliances are turned off). It may be the same phrase or it may be a pattern of thought. Doing this occasionally is manageable but if it is happening all the time it is best to talk it through with someone. If these thoughts and behaviours are upsetting you they are something to talk to a professional about. This will help to stop them interfering with your life, and help you to feel better.

Dramatic mood changes

Our moods go up and down. However, if you feel consistently down, sad or angry, and the feelings last more than two to four weeks, talk them through with a trusted professional. If you have reached the stage of feeling worthless, or you cannot see your way out of a situation, ask for help today. Students are often surprised that feeling angry all the time may also be a sign of depression. If you have mood swings that seem quite extreme, and cause problems in relationships or in your daily life, then ask for help. This is especially important if they lead to self-harmful behaviours, such as cutting, taking tablets, or drinking too much alcohol. The module titled: Self-harm and suicidal thoughts offers further support and information.

Sleep problems

Sleep is one of the first things to go wrong when we feel stressed, anxious or low. We may struggle to fall asleep, wake throughout the night, or wake in the early hours. Whilst it can be normal to sleep badly before a big day such as an exam or interview, it is not healthy for sleep disturbance to continue for weeks at a time. If this is happening to you, try not to use alcohol or drugs to 'self-medicate', as these may make the problem worse. Sleep can be fixed in healthy ways, but it can take a little time. Also, see a GP if you have felt tired all the time for more than a month. Good sleep will mean better moods and overall quality of life. It also helps with good academic performance. This is explored more thoroughly in the module titled: Study-life balance.

Appetite changes

Sensible, healthy eating is often forgotten when we feel stressed for a prolonged period. We may eat too much or too little. We may eat the wrong things because they are convenient, or give us a quick fix of energy. Whilst we can all manage with a poor diet for a few days, it is best to seek help if you are eating very little, or a very restricted diet. Also seek help if you eat a lot in one sitting then make yourself sick, for weeks at a time. Healthy eating is also explored in the module titled: Eating well.

Feeling isolated or lonely

Being at university can mean leaving behind friends and family. It can also mean feeling a bit lost and homesick or even isolated. Whilst all these feelings are common and are likely to fade as you settle in, a few students will feel isolated and lonely much of the time. If you are finding it hard to make friends or socialise, ask for support.

Focus, motivation and concentration issues

Many students are easily distracted, especially if they work in a busy environment, or don't feel enthusiastic about their current topic. However, in general if you are studying a subject that engages you and you are still struggling to focus, then it may be worth talking through with your tutor or a counsellor. This is especially true if it continues for more than two to four weeks. If your attention and concentration problems are long-standing (for example since you were a child) then you may need to discuss this with a GP. It may also be helpful to get advice and study support from your university's disability support team.

Suicidal thoughts and self-harm

Suicidal feelings and self-harm behaviours will be covered in detail in the module titled: Self-harm and suicidal thoughts. These feelings can occasionally affect students alongside the other feelings described in this activity. Students who feel very down, flat or angry, or who have bad anxiety, may start to think that life is too hard. Sometimes they would rather not be here, or think it would be 'easier' for others if they were no longer around. These suicidal feelings are exhausting and frightening. They should be talked through with a compassionate professional, such as a doctor, psychologist or counsellor. If you are hurting yourself physically, for example by cutting or burning your skin, or taking too many tablets, then please talk to someone about your distress and self-harm behaviour (whether or not you have suicidal thoughts and feelings). There is much that can be done to help you.

 Useful links Useful links

If your life is in danger, call 000. If you need crisis support or help with suicidal thoughts, ring Lifeline on 13 11 14 or connect with them via their website – https://www.lifeline.org.au.

ReachOut recommends the Calm Harm app, which provides tasks that help you resist or manage the urge to self-harm. Check out their other app recommendations at the same time.

Lifeline and the Suicide Call Back Service are national support services offering phone and online counselling for those struggling with suicidal thoughts and self-harm.

Beyond Now and ReMinder are apps you can use to develop a suicide safety plan – something that helps you feel safe when you are experiencing suicidal thoughts.

 Peer support Peer support

Think about your friends and housemates. Have you noticed or been worried about these emotions and behaviours in them?

If so, you might like to gently approach the subject by saying you are a little worried about them and want to check they are OK. You could invite them to join you in an activity. If you are not confident about speaking to them directly, take advice from a member of university staff that you trust, like a counselling team member, about what to do next.
