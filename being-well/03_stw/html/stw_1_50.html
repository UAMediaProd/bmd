When things aren't going according to plan, there are lots of ways your university can offer you support. Too many students don't use these systems because they feel that they should be able to cope alone. Nothing could be further from the truth; no one would or should judge you for a situation you have found yourself in and which isn't in your control. Similarly, no one would or should judge you for seeking help if you are struggling with academic pressure, even if you don't have a diagnosed mental health disorder.

Different universities might have slightly different processes or different systems supporting these processes or, indeed, different names for similar processes.

Extenuating circumstances
  • 'Extenuating circumstances' – or 'mitigating circumstances' – refers to a situation where something has happened outside of your control that has directly impacted on your capacity to study. It could be physical ill health, bereavement or a mental health problem, such as problematic anxiety or depression
  • It allows the university to consider these circumstances to ensure you are not prejudiced in an assessment. Such considerations are typically undertaken by the head of school staff and your lecturer, and personal information is not routinely shared beyond that
  • Some sort of evidence is usually required, e.g. a note from your doctor or, sometimes, university counsellor or mental health advisor.

Extension
  • This would provide the university with an opportunity to agree an extension to a deadline for a piece of work, or a set of work, should your capacity to study have been affected in similar circumstances to extenuating or mitigating circumstances
  • Evidence is usually required to support such an application.

Reasonable adjustments
  • Reasonable adjustments are when the university can vary their usual processes to accommodate a particular set of circumstances or processes – for example, supporting a student with a disability
  • This might lead to a learning support plan, where flexible deadlines for work are routinely agreed, or where adjustments for examinations can be made, such as sitting in a separate room or having a 'scribe' (someone who will write for you on your instruction)
  • Evidence would be required for the university to make reasonable adjustments, and these would be fully discussed with you in advance.

Changing study load and deferment
  • This happens when you wish, or need, to change or interrupt your studies for a period of time, from full-time to part-time, taking a leave of absence or deferring studies from a month to a year, for example
  • This may be because of a variety of reasons, but could include mental and physical wellbeing, and situations where other changes would be insufficient. It effectively 'stops the clock' on your studies, but might mean having to return within a certain time and with another cohort of students
  • The level of evidence required in this case is often less than for other routes.

 Useful links Useful links

The following links might offer additional helpful sources of support and information if you feel you are struggling with your mental health whilst studying:

  • Lifeline is a national charity that provides 24-hour crisis support and suicide prevention services to Australians experiencing a personal crisis
  • Reach Out provides practical support, tools and tips for young people managing all types of mental health issues. Check out this article on stress and anxiety for an example
  • Beyond Blue provides you with support if you are feeling low or overwhelmed at university
  • The Black Dog Institute provides information, research and support tools and apps to help people seek the right support for their mental health.

As well as student services, there are often other sources of support available at universities, such as pastoral support from university staff in student support roles, and academic counsellors. These services can play a vital role in enabling you to meet your potential. However, many students find that by putting the right study strategies in place and ensuring good levels of self-care, they can build resilience and more effective ways of coping with problems as they come along.

At some universities, peer support is available from other students through supported programmes. This can be organised within schools, faculties or residential accommodation. These are excellent opportunities to get advice from other students who have already been through the process and can provide hints and tips about how to get things done and study techniques, or just be someone to talk to. Check if your university offers peer support opportunities, and sign up if it does!
