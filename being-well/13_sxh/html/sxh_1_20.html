There are a lot of different contraception options out there. Some use hormones, some are short-term solutions while others are long-lasting, some are free and others you may have to pay for. Thinking about your own situation and preferences will help you decide which is best for you.

 Contraceptive ring

A plastic ring that is inserted into the vagina. Sits up around the cervix for three weeks at a time and releases hormones to prevent pregnancy.

Pros: Put in or remove when you want to.

Cons: Can take some practice to insert correctly, and does not protect from STIs.

 Contraceptive injection

Injections you get once every three months to prevent pregnancy.

Pros: Long-lasting, and you don't have to take a pill every day.

Cons: Need to go to a clinic every three months for injections. There is some suggestion that bones can be weakened if used for a long period of time. Does not protect against STIs.

 Emergency contraception ('morning after' pill)

A pill that can be taken up to 72 hours after unprotected sex to prevent pregnancy.

Pros: Back-up option if you forget to use protection or if a condom breaks. Can be sourced from your GP or without a prescription from most pharmacies.

Cons: Needs to be taken as soon as possible. This is a back-up that shouldn't replace using regular contraceptive methods. Nausea is a possible side-effect. Does not protect against STIs.

 Latex or nitrile gloves

Swap gloves between touching a partner's genitals and your own to prevent body fluids from mixing. Popular for lesbian and queer sex.

Pros: Cheap and easy to find. Protects cuts on your hands too.

Cons: Can be a bit awkward at first, but easy once you get used to it. Just find gloves of the right size and material for you.

 Internal condoms

A cylinder-shaped barrier that goes inside the vagina or anus.

Pros: The wearer is in control. Can be inserted in advance.

Cons: Can be hard to find and expensive.

 PEP

If you think you have been exposed to HIV you can take a course of PEP medication to prevent you from getting infected with the virus.

Pros: A good back-up option that is effective if started within 72 hours of exposure.

Cons: You need to go to a hospital or sexual health clinic to access PEP. Needs to be taken correctly for several weeks. Does not offer protection from other STIs.

 PrEP

Medication you take every day to prevent you from getting HIV if you are exposed to the virus.

Pros: Puts you in charge. Don't need to rely on remembering a condom in the moment (although PrEP won't protect you from other STIs).

Cons: Some side-effects. You have to remember to take your medication daily.
